🌊 The River
Where Memphis began
Cobblestones
Landmark
Riverside Dr, Court Ave to Beale St
The only surviving 19th-century cobblestone river landing in North America — over 900,000 stones and 100+ iron mooring fixtures from the steamboat era. Paving began 1859.
Mud Island River Park
Park
125 N Front St
A peninsula formed in 1912 around the abandoned warship USS Amphitrite. Features a 5-block-long scale model of the lower Mississippi and an amphitheater.
🌐 Website
Frisco & Harahan Bridges
Bridge
235 Virginia Ave W
The Frisco Bridge (1892) was the first bridge across the lower Mississippi. The Harahan's north roadway became the Big River Crossing bike/pedestrian path in 2016.
🌐 Website
Tom Lee Park
Park
Riverside Dr at Beale St
Named for Tom Lee, who saved 32 passengers from the capsized steamer M.E. Norman in 1925 despite being unable to swim. A monument with 32 lights honors each survivor.
🌐 Website
🏺 Native Heritage
Before Memphis
Chickasaw Heritage Park
Archaeological
351 Metal Museum Dr
Remains of a large Mississippian Culture settlement on the bluffs. Also home to the National Ornamental Metal Museum.
🌐 Website
Chucalissa Museum
Museum
T.O. Fuller State Park
Prehistoric village site occupied 1000–1500 CE by the Mississippian Culture. Reconstructed houses and museum run by the University of Memphis.
🌐 Website
🏛 Antebellum
1819–1861
Cotton Row
District
Front St, Gayoso to Jefferson
Memphis became the world's largest inland cotton market by the 1850s. At its peak, over 400,000 bales passed through Cotton Row annually.
Calvary Episcopal Church
1843
102 N Second St
Memphis's oldest building in continuous use. Gothic Revival design, locally made brick. Survived yellow fever, the Civil War, and urban renewal.
🌐 Website
Magevney House
1833
198 Adams Ave
Oldest surviving downtown home (1833), built by Irish immigrant Eugene Magevney who hosted the city's first Catholic Mass here.
Elmwood Cemetery
1852
824 S Dudley St
Garden cemetery. Resting place of 75,000+ people including Civil War generals, yellow fever victims, blues musicians, and 14 mayors.
🌐 Website
⚔️ Civil War
1861–1865
Battle of Memphis
June 6, 1862
Mississippi River bluffs
Thousands watched from the bluffs as the Union fleet destroyed the Confederate River Defense Fleet in just 90 minutes. Memphis fell without a land fight.
Fort Pickering
Garrison
351 Metal Museum Dr
Major Union garrison during the Civil War. Became a refuge for thousands of formerly enslaved people who fled behind Union lines.
Memphis National Cemetery
40,000+
3568 Townes Ave
Final resting place of 40,000+ veterans, including 8,800+ unknown Union dead from Shiloh, Corinth, and other battles.
🌐 Website
✨ Gilded Age
1865–1908
Court Square
1876
N Main St & Court Ave
Memphis's oldest public space where Davy Crockett and Sam Houston once lounged. The Hebe Fountain (1876) is one of America's oldest cast-iron fountains.
Woodruff-Fontaine House
1870
680 Adams Ave · Victorian Village
French Victorian mansion now a house museum showcasing Gilded Age elegance with period furnishings.
🌐 Website
Mallory-Neely House
Italianate
652 Adams Ave
25 rooms of original furnishings spanning four generations — a remarkably preserved embodiment of Memphis's Gilded Age.
Overton Park
1906
2080 Poplar Ave
Designed by George Kessler. Houses the Memphis Zoo, Brooks Museum, and the Overton Park Shell amphitheater.
🌐 Website
🎺 Beale Street
The heart of Black Memphis
Beale Street
1841
Beale St Historic District
First laid out in 1841, became the 'Negro Main Street of America' — eclipsing Harlem as the hub of Black business, music, and culture.
🌐 Website
Beale Street Baptist Church
1885
379 Beale St
First brick church in the South built by and for former slaves. Ida B. Wells published her anti-lynching newspaper from its basement.
W.C. Handy House
1912–18
352 Beale St
Home of the 'Father of the Blues,' where he composed 'Memphis Blues' and 'St. Louis Blues.' The house was moved from South Memphis to Beale Street.
Old Daisy Theater
1917
329 Beale St
One of the earliest and most elegant Black theaters. Part of old Saloon Row's transformation into a theater district.
🏛 Crump Era
1909–1954
Orpheum Theater
1928
203 S Main St
Originally the Grand Opera House (1890), rebuilt 1928 in lavish movie-palace style. Said to be haunted by a girl named Mary who died in a 1920s fire.
🌐 Website
Peabody Hotel
1925
149 Union Ave
Rebuilt 1925. Famous for the March of the Peabody Ducks tradition since 1933 and its magnificent travertine lobby.
🌐 Website
Pink Palace Museum
Museum
3050 Central Ave
Pink marble mansion of Piggly Wiggly founder Clarence Saunders, who invented the self-service grocery store (1916). Became a museum in 1930.
🌐 Website
Sears Crosstown Concourse
1927
1350 Concourse Ave
Art deco Sears building (1927), largest in Memphis when built. Brilliantly repurposed as a vertical urban village of arts, education, and housing.
🌐 Website
Arcade Restaurant
Since 1919
540 S Main St
Memphis's oldest restaurant. Elvis was a regular — always the last booth on the left.
🌐 Website
🎸 Rock'n'Roll
1954–1976 · The birthplace
Sun Studio
Historic Landmark
706 Union Ave
Sam Phillips opened in 1950. Produced the first rock'n'roll single (1951) and launched Elvis, Johnny Cash, Jerry Lee Lewis, and Carl Perkins.
🌐 Website
Graceland
Elvis · 1957
3764 Elvis Presley Blvd
Purchased by Elvis in 1957 for $100,000 at age 22. Open to the public since 1982 — one of the most-visited homes in America.
🌐 Website
Stax Museum
Soul
926 McLemore Ave
Stax Records was founded in an old movie theater. Otis Redding, Isaac Hayes, and the Staple Singers all recorded here.
🌐 Website
Overton Park Shell
1936 · WPA
1928 Poplar Ave
WPA amphitheater where Elvis gave his first paid public concert on July 30, 1954. One of few surviving WPA amphitheaters in America.
🌐 Website
Lansky Brothers
1946
126 Beale St
'Clothier to the King' — Elvis bought his flashy stage outfits here starting as a broke teenager on layaway.

✊ Civil Rights
The African American experience
Mason Temple / COGIC
April 3, 1968
938 Mason St
MLK delivered his prophetic "I've Been to the Mountaintop" speech here — the night before his assassination at the Lorraine Motel.
Withers Collection Museum
Photography
333 Beale St
Studio of photojournalist Ernest C. Withers, who documented the civil rights movement including the Emmett Till trial and the 1968 sanitation strike.
🌐 Website
LeMoyne-Owen College
HBCU · 1862
807 Walker Ave
One of the oldest HBCUs in the South (roots to 1862). Central to Memphis's civil rights movement.
🌐 Website
WDIA Radio
1948
2074 Union Ave
America's first all-Black-programmed radio station. Its signal reached a million listeners and launched B.B. King and Rufus Thomas.
