🌊 The River

Where Memphis began
🪨 Landmark

Cobblestones

Riverside Dr, Court Ave to Beale St

The only surviving 19th-century cobblestone river landing in North America — over 900,000 stones from the steamboat era.

📍 Map
🏝 Park

Mud Island River Park

125 N Front St

Peninsula formed in 1912 around the abandoned warship USS Amphitrite. Features a 5-block-long scale model of the lower Mississippi.

Website ↗📍
🌉 Bridge

Frisco & Harahan Bridges

235 Virginia Ave W

The Frisco (1892) was the first bridge across the lower Mississippi. The Harahan's north roadway became the Big River Crossing bike path in 2016.

Website ↗📍
🏞 Park

Tom Lee Park

Riverside Dr at Beale St

Named for Tom Lee, who saved 32 passengers from the capsized steamer M.E. Norman in 1925 despite being unable to swim.

Website ↗📍

🏺 Native Heritage

Before Memphis
🗿 Archaeological

Chickasaw Heritage Park

351 Metal Museum Dr

Remains of a Mississippian Culture settlement on the bluffs. Also home to the National Ornamental Metal Museum.

Website ↗📍
🏛 Museum

Chucalissa Museum

T.O. Fuller State Park

Prehistoric village and mound complex (1000–1500 CE). Reconstructed houses and museum run by the University of Memphis.

Website ↗📍

🏛 Antebellum Memphis

1819–1860
🏘 District

Cotton Row

Front St, Gayoso to Jefferson

Memphis became the world's largest inland cotton market. Over 400,000 bales passed through annually at peak.

📍 Map
⛪ Church

Calvary Episcopal Church

102 N Second St

Memphis's oldest public building in continuous use (1843). Survived yellow fever, Civil War, and urban renewal.

Website ↗📍
🏠 House

Magevney House

198 Adams Ave

Oldest surviving downtown home (1833). Irish immigrant Eugene Magevney hosted the city's first Catholic Mass here.

Website ↗📍
⚰️ Cemetery

Elmwood Cemetery

824 S Dudley St

Garden cemetery (1852). Resting place of 75,000+ people including Civil War generals, yellow fever victims, and 14 mayors.

Website ↗📍
🏠 House

Hunt-Phelan House

533 Beale St

Designed by Robert Mills (Washington Monument architect). Completed 1832, served as Grant's HQ during the Civil War.

📍 Map

⚔️ Civil War

1861–1865
⚓ Battle

Battle of Memphis

Mississippi River bluffs

June 6, 1862: the Union fleet destroyed the Confederate navy in just 90 minutes as thousands watched from the bluffs.

📍 Map
🏰 Fort

Fort Pickering

351 Metal Museum Dr

Major Union garrison during the war. Became a refuge for thousands of formerly enslaved people who fled to freedom.

📍 Map
⚰️ Cemetery

Memphis National Cemetery

3568 Townes Ave

Final resting place of 40,000+ veterans, including 8,800+ unknown Union dead from Shiloh and Corinth.

Website ↗📍

✨ Gilded Age

1865–1908
⛲ Square

Court Square

N Main St & Court Ave

Memphis's oldest public space — Davy Crockett and Sam Houston once lounged here. The Hebe Fountain (1876) still stands.

📍 Map
🏠 House

Woodruff-Fontaine House

680 Adams Ave

French Victorian mansion (1870) in Victorian Village. Now a house museum of Gilded Age elegance.

Website ↗📍
🏠 House

Mallory-Neely House

652 Adams Ave

25 rooms of original furnishings spanning four generations. A remarkably preserved Italianate mansion.

Website ↗📍
🌳 Park

Overton Park

2080 Poplar Ave

Designed by George Kessler (1906). Houses the Memphis Zoo, Brooks Museum, and Overton Park Shell.

Website ↗📍
🏗 Building

Porter Building

10 N Main St

Memphis's first skyscraper (1895). People lined up just to ride the elevator — a novelty at the time.

📍 Map
🏭 Industrial

Tennessee Brewery

495 Tennessee St

Castle-like building (1877–1890), once the largest brewery in the South. Now loft apartments preserving the façade.

📍 Map

🎺 Beale Street

The heart of Black Memphis
🎵 District

Beale Street

Beale St Historic District

First laid out in 1841, became the 'Negro Main Street of America' — eclipsing even Harlem as the hub of Black culture.

Website ↗📍
⛪ Church

Beale Street Baptist Church

379 Beale St

First brick church in the South built by former slaves (1885). Ida B. Wells published her anti-lynching newspaper from its basement.

📍 Map
🌳 Park

Church Park

Beale & Fourth Sts

Built 1899 by Robert R. Church Sr. The only park for African Americans at the time, with a 2,000-seat auditorium.

📍 Map
🎵 Music

W.C. Handy House

352 Beale St

Home of the 'Father of the Blues' (1912–18), where he composed 'Memphis Blues' and 'St. Louis Blues.'

📍 Map
🎭 Theater

Old Daisy Theater

329 Beale St

One of the earliest and most elegant Black theaters (1917), part of Saloon Row's transformation into a theater district.

📍 Map

🏛 Crump Era

1909–1954
🎭 Theater

Orpheum Theater

203 S Main St

Originally the Grand Opera House (1890), rebuilt 1928 in lavish movie-palace style. Said to be haunted by a girl named Mary.

Website ↗📍
🏨 Hotel

Peabody Hotel

149 Union Ave

Rebuilt 1925. Famous for the March of the Peabody Ducks tradition since 1933 and its magnificent travertine lobby.

Website ↗📍
🏛 Museum

Pink Palace Museum

3050 Central Ave

Pink marble mansion of Piggly Wiggly founder Clarence Saunders, who invented the self-service grocery store (1916). Museum since 1930.

Website ↗📍
🏗 Building

Sears Crosstown / Concourse

1350 Concourse Ave

Art deco Sears building (1927), repurposed as Crosstown Concourse — a vertical village of arts, education, and housing.

Website ↗📍
🍽 Restaurant

Arcade Restaurant

540 S Main St

Memphis's oldest restaurant, open since 1919. Elvis was a regular — always the last booth on the left.

Website ↗📍
🏛 Government

Shelby County Courthouse

140 Adams Ave

Neoclassical courthouse (1906–09). Before it, courts met in a log cabin at Court Square.

📍 Map

🎸 Rock'n'Roll

1954–1976
🎙 Studio

Sun Studio

706 Union Ave

Sam Phillips's studio produced the first rock'n'roll single (1951) and launched Elvis, Johnny Cash, Jerry Lee Lewis. National Historic Landmark.

Website ↗📍
🏠 Landmark

Graceland

3764 Elvis Presley Blvd

Purchased by Elvis in 1957 for $100,000 at age 22. Open since 1982 — one of America's most-visited homes.

Website ↗📍
🎵 Museum

Stax Museum

926 McLemore Ave

Where Otis Redding, Isaac Hayes, and the Staple Singers recorded. Founded in an old movie theater by siblings Jim Stewart and Estelle Axton.

Website ↗📍
🎤 Venue

Overton Park Shell

1928 Poplar Ave

WPA amphitheater (1936) where Elvis gave his first paid public concert on July 30, 1954.

Website ↗📍
👔 Shop

Lansky Brothers

126 Beale St

'Clothier to the King' — Elvis bought his flashy stage outfits here starting as a broke teenager on layaway.

Website ↗📍
🏠 Landmark

Lauderdale Courts

BB King Blvd

1938 WPA housing where the Presleys lived 1949–53. Young Elvis walked from here to Beale Street to absorb the music.

📍 Map

✊ Civil Rights

The African American experience
⛪ Temple

Mason Temple / COGIC

938 Mason St

MLK delivered 'I've Been to the Mountaintop' here on April 3, 1968 — the night before his assassination.

📍 Map
📷 Museum

Withers Collection Museum

333 Beale St

Studio of photographer Ernest C. Withers who documented the civil rights movement, including the Emmett Till trial.

Website ↗📍
🎓 College

LeMoyne-Owen College

807 Walker Ave

One of the oldest HBCUs in the South (roots to 1862). Central to Memphis's civil rights movement.

Website ↗📍
📻 Radio

WDIA Radio Station

2074 Union Ave

America's first all-Black-programmed station (1948). Launched B.B. King and Rufus Thomas, reaching a million listeners.

📍 Map
⚰️ Cemetery

Zion Christian Cemetery

1426 S Parkway E

Memphis's oldest African American cemetery (1873). Resting place of Ida B. Wells's parents and early community leaders.

📍 Map

Collierville Town Square

Town Square, Collierville

Laid out 1866. The town endured three Civil War battles and was rebuilt around this charming central square.

Davies Manor
